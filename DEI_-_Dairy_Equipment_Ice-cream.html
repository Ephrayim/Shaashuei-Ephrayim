DEI

By Ephrayim Baskin  |  Shavuos 5785 — June 2025

DEI – Dairy Equipment Ice-cream (why, what did you think I was going to write about?) – and whether it can be consumed by someone who is careful to only eat Cholov Yisroel is the second most common question I get about ice cream. The most common question, of course, is if I am related to Baskin-Robbins. It goes beyond just my name, as I have a notoriously sweet tooth and my poor kids have many a time gone to the freezer to look for that yummy ice cream treat they have been saving, only to find an empty wrapper and their dad with a creamily satisfied grin.

So, to churn away all questions: Baskin- Robbins was founded in 1945 by two brothers-in-law, Burt Baskin and Irv Robbins (Burt married Irv’s sister). Both were Jewish – Irv even whipped out some of his saved up Bar Mitzvah money to start the business. It was a sweet investment indeed. Burt’s father, Harold, came to the USA from Russia, and if we go back a couple generations on genealogy.com, we find an ancestor born in the late 1700s, Bentzion Leib Baskin – sounds pretty Jewish. In fact, there seems to be a cool connection between Jewish entrepreneurs and ice cream companies, such as Häagen- Dazs (Polish immigrants Reuben and Rose Mattus) as well as Ben and Jerry’s (whose political opinions might not be as cool).

On my side, I can trace back to one Refoel “Follie” Baskin from Lithuania – father of Moshe, father of Zelig, father of Follie (again), father of Sharai (don’t ask, his Hebrew name is another article in itself), father of Ephrayim (ice-cream devourer), father of Bassie and Follie (again) and Kovi and Chaya (the same kids missing their ice cream in paragraph 1).

Unfortunately, I can’t find a connection between the two Baskins other than that they were Jewish and came from Russia. A connection so tenuous, that even the Baskin- Robbins attendant wouldn’t give me a free drink when I tried to present it to him. What a chutzpah.

Anyway, back to the creamy question: DEI. In the 2024/25 Kosher Australia Food Guide there are more than 80 different ice creams and desserts that have been designated with the DE status, possibly the highest DE percentage of any food group (Yankel, am I right?). Why is there so much DE in I (dairy equipment in ice-cream) – I mean it’s cold, right? And are all DE ice creams the same (putting the DEI in DEI)? Let’s take a quick glaze into ice cream manufacturing.

Although there are various sizes of factories, most will follow the same general process.

The first part is mixing and dissolving the base ingredients. Ice cream will need to have a certain percentage of cream followed by other dairy ingredients (proteins, minerals, lactose), sugar, sugar, sugar (or at least sweeteners), emulsifiers (chemicals to bind the fats and non-fat parts of the ice cream – homemade ice cream will usually use egg to do this), stabilisers (chemicals that will bind water and increase the viscosity to provide both texture as well as control the growth of ice-crystals), and flavours and colours. Other add-ins are sprinkled in later.

For non-dairy ice cream, it is almost the same as the above, however they will use a non-dairy fat, like coconut or palm oil, and a non-dairy source of protein, minerals etc., which might be provided from soy or the like. The mixing is usually done in a large vat and is heated to around 50–60°C. Then all these ingredients need to be pasteurised, dissolved and homogenised. Smoothed. Although normal milk is pasteurised at 72°C, when the fat content is greater than 10% or contains sweetness or is concentrated (which would be the case for ice cream base), the minimum requirement is 75°C because pathogens can survive better in fattier foods. Furthermore, to ensure proper dissolving of ingredients, ice cream factories can reach up to 85°C in this part of the process. (There is a hot- cream pun opportunity somewhere here, but the article deadline is approaching.)

Following this, the product is rapidly chilled to less than 5°C (to let the emulsifiers and stabilisers do their work). Thereafter, it is put through a continuous freezing process where air is whipped into the ice cream mix to give it volume and fluff, chilled further to -3° to -6°C. Most ice cream companies are literally selling you air.

Dry ingredients and syrups are then added, then the ice cream is pumped to various packaging lines to make tubs, cones and moulded stick varieties using extrusion technology and specialised moulding machines.

Icy poles will be made in a similar fashion, minus the continuous freezing step and the fat addition. Depending on what ingredients are used, pasteurisation is not always required.

Because dairy ice cream, non-dairy ice cream and even icy poles use such similar manufacturing methods, they will commonly share factory equipment – hence the great number of dairy equipment varieties. As there is heating involved, well above yad soledes bo (the minimum temperature for halachic “flavour” to be transmitted to the vessels), any non-dairy product shared on that equipment will become considered Dairy Equipment.

The question then is what does DE practically mean?

The Kosher Australia Food Guide gives us a quick synopsis on DE:

Regarding the DAIRY EQUIPMENT designation, otherwise pareve products with no dairy ingredients that have been produced on equipment that is shared with dairy products without an adequate koshering beforehand, have been marked as DE. Such products may not necessarily attain dairy status and may still be considered pareve under certain conditions. Customs and details vary regarding these conditions but in brief:

a. DE products should not be eaten together with meat (fleishik) foods;

b. DE products may be eaten separately, without the usual waiting period between meat and dairy foods – even at the same meal;

c. They may be used (even hot) with meat utensils;

d. Many people who use only Cholov Yisroel dairy products do not use DE products where the dairy is not Cholov Yisroel.

You can ask any young rabbi the details and discussions of points A through C, and they might start talking about nut bars (or “nut bar nut”) and the such, but we are here to scoop the details regarding point D. There are many articles that go into the background and modern details of Cholov Yisroel, so for the purpose of this article I am only going to give an outline:

Biblically, as long as the majority of milking animals in an area are kosher, there would be no concern in purchasing the milk.

The Talmud (Avodah Zoro 35b) describes that Chazal were concerned about non-kosher milk being mixed in with kosher milk and required that the milk be supervised by a Jewish person.

The Chossom Sofer (Yoreh Deah 2:107) stresses that as it was a gezeirah (a decree), even though in some modern societies this concern doesn’t exist, it is nonetheless treated as not kosher.

The Pri Chodosh (Yoreh Deah 115:6) argues that the Chazal never decreed. Rather it was just a warning for the times and where no such concern exists we need not be concerned. This position is rejected on the whole.

R’ Moshe Feinstein (Igros Moshe, Yoreh Deah 1:47) outlines his famous leniency for first world countries, where he doesn’t dispel the Chossom Sofer’s position, but suggests that due to the strong governmental systems and regulations on the dairy industry in a first world country, it is as if we have supervised the milk (using a Talmudic concept of “Ana Sahadi”).

However, from a DE perspective, the important part is what the Rama states in Shulchan Oruch (YD 115:1): Even if there are no non-kosher animals in the herd, we still treat the milk as non-kosher if it hasn’t been supervised, and even more so any vessel used to cook the unsupervised milk is considered to be as if non-kosher has been cooked in it.

Based on all of this, we have three general approaches to Dairy Equipment products:

1. Those who follow R’ Moshe’s leniency would consider DE as per points A–C in the snippet from the kosher guide above.

2. Those who reject this leniency will consider DE as if it has been cooked on non-kosher equipment.

3. Then we have the middle ground (perhaps a bit more pareve in their approach?) – those who are stringent to only eat Cholov Yisroel products and are lenient for DE (Rav Yosef Henkin Teshuvos Ibra 43, Shulchan HaLevi 22:5, Shaarim Metzuim B’Halacha 38:12). Furthermore there is room to be lenient in scenarios of great loss (Darkei Teshuva 115:18).

Most Chabad chassidim will follow the approach of the Chossom Sofer and this seems to be the approach of the Rebbe as well (Igros Kodesh III p. 366). Accordingly, DE products would be treated like products cooked on non-kosher equipment.

Although the dairy industry is rigorous in its cleaning procedures, the cleans aren’t necessarily performed under the conditions required for kashering. Cleaning is usually done with a caustic solution and, as a rule of thumb, at the same temperature to which the product has been exposed. Whether such liquids and such temperatures are considered a kashering is debatable and most kosher agencies won’t rely on this l’chatchilah. The water wash afterwards is sometimes hot enough to reach temperatures akin to kashering, however usually there is a “hot water loop” which can cause issues (I can loop back to this in another article I think). As such, to certify these products they are usually marked as dairy equipment.

However, this is in the first instance (which to be honest is most of the time when consuming ice cream, even for people with a sweet tooth like me). In a scenario of b’dieved (e.g. when you make a visit to the ED with one of the kids and they offer a DE icy pole) I would encourage you to reach out to your local Orthodox Rabbi. Which leads me to the all encompassing and generic kosher article non-conclusion conclusion, leaving you with more questions than answers and in true Aussie style hand-balling any further queries to your local Orthodox Rabbi (sorry Rabbi Lesches).
